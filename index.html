How Defuse Works

Three principles. One platform.

Every issue. Every angle. Concise enough to read in the time it takes to scroll through a single news article.

Transparency

Every voice on Defuse is clearly identified on the political spectrum. No hidden agendas, no pretending to be neutral. You always know where someone stands — and you can decide for yourself.

Curated Diversity

No matter how hard you try, your X page is not curated to take in events and issues with balance. We can do it for you.

Steel-manning

The opposite of a "straw man." Our AI doesn't debate or judge — it strengthens. For every perspective, Defuse generates the most charitable interpretation, so you can engage with the best version of ideas you may otherwise disagree with.

The Method

How we score the political spectrum.

Every commentator on Defuse is mapped across five dimensions on a 1–10 scale. No single number can capture a person's politics — that's exactly why we use five.

The Five Dimensions

Domestic Policy (1–10)

1 = Market-driven / non-interventionist. Favors deregulation, low taxes, privatization, school choice, market-based healthcare, and minimal government involvement in domestic affairs.

10 = Distributive / government involvement. Favors universal programs, public education investment, single-payer healthcare, strong safety nets, wealth taxes, and active government role in domestic life.

Foreign Policy (1–10)

1 = Hawkish / interventionist. Supports a strong military posture, alliances like NATO, forward-deployed forces, and willingness to use force to advance U.S. interests.

10 = Dovish / non-interventionist. Skeptical of military action, favors diplomacy, opposes foreign entanglements, and prioritizes domestic spending over defense.

Trade (1–10)

1 = Free trade. Supports open markets, low tariffs, international trade agreements, and globalized supply chains.

10 = Protectionist. Favors tariffs, reshoring, industrial policy, "Buy American" mandates, and skepticism of trade deals.

Cultural (1–10)

1 = Culturally conservative / traditionalist. Emphasizes religious values, traditional institutions, national identity, and skepticism of progressive social movements.

10 = Culturally progressive. Prioritizes social justice, identity-based equity, secular governance, and expanding rights for marginalized groups.

Orthodoxy (1–10)

1 = Heterodox / independent. Breaks with their "expected" side frequently. Holds views that don't fit neatly into left or right. Willing to criticize their own tribe.

10 = Party-line / orthodox. Reliably aligns with their party or ideological camp on nearly every issue. Rarely breaks ranks.

How scores are assigned

Scores are based on each commentator's publicly stated positions — in their writing, shows, podcasts, social media, and interviews. We look at patterns, not one-off quotes. A commentator's score reflects the weight of their overall body of work, not a single hot take.

The goal is to give you a meaningful map of where people stand relative to each other across multiple dimensions, so that a libertarian looks different from a neoconservative, and a progressive looks different from a socialist. Over time, we are hopeful to use an LLM for more precise "scores" — but for now, treat these as imperfect but directionally accurate.

Political Archetypes

Spider charts make it easy to see at a glance how someone's politics are shaped. Here are some common political archetypes and what their charts look like.

The Libertarian

Free markets, free trade, non-interventionist, culturally moderate, and deeply heterodox. Doesn't fit neatly on either team.

The Neoconservative

Market-oriented domestic policy, hawkish on foreign policy, free trade, culturally moderate-right, and fairly party-line.

The MAGA Populist

Mixed on domestic policy, non-interventionist, highly protectionist, culturally traditional, and fiercely party-line.

The Progressive

Strongly redistributive, dovish, mixed on trade, culturally progressive, and fairly party-line.

The Centrist / Pragmatist

Moderate across every dimension and highly heterodox. The chart is small and symmetrical — no strong pull in any direction.

The Left Populist

Strongly redistributive, dovish, protectionist, culturally progressive, and heterodox — breaks with the Democratic establishment.

A spider chart is a fingerprint

Each axis extends from the center (1) to the outer edge (10). The filled shape shows a commentator's profile at a glance. A large, spiky shape means someone holds strong views across multiple dimensions. A small, compact shape near the center means someone is moderate or mixed.

The most interesting shapes are the asymmetric ones — they reveal the people who defy simple categorization. A commentator who is far-right on domestic policy but progressive on culture, or hawkish on foreign policy but protectionist on trade, will have a distinctive shape that immediately tells you they're not a cookie-cutter partisan.

About

Built by the politically homeless.

Defuse was started by two people who listen to Pod Save America on a Monday and The Ben Shapiro Show on a Tuesday — not out of confusion, but out of curiosity. We wanted to hear the smartest version of every argument, not just the ones our algorithm picked for us.

We kept coming back to the same thought experiment: what would happen if you took the sharpest voices across the political spectrum and stranded them on a desert island together? No audiences to perform for. No engagement metrics. No reason to dunk on each other. Just the arguments.

We think they might actually like each other.

That's a naive thought, and we know it. But Defuse was built on the belief that naivety might be exactly what political media needs right now. The entire industry is optimized to make you angry, afraid, and certain that the other side is either stupid or evil. We think that's a design flaw, not a feature.

So we built something different. Defuse puts left, center, and right side by side — on the same issue, on the same page. Every take gets a steel-man: the most charitable, intellectually honest interpretation, even for ideas you disagree with. Especially for ideas you disagree with.

We're not centrists. We're not "both sides" people. We have our own views. But we believe you can hold strong convictions and still think the other side has something worth hearing. That's not weakness. That's the whole point.

Who's included — and who's not.

We curated roughly 150 of the most influential political commentators in the United States. We think diversity of opinion is important — essential, actually. That's the whole premise of this project.

But we also believe a working Overton window should be wide, not infinite. There's a difference between a perspective that challenges your assumptions and one that poisons the conversation. We tried to draw that line thoughtfully, and that may explain why certain political commentators have been intentionally left off.

If you think we got it wrong — either by including someone we shouldn't have or leaving out someone we should — don't be afraid to let us know. Similarly, if you are part of the commentariat and would like to push back on your political score, we want to hear about it.

We want to grow this.

Defuse is two people and an idea. We want it to be more. If you're a journalist, a developer, a designer, a political science nerd, or just someone who's tired of being told what to think — we want to hear from you.

If any part of this resonates, reach out. We're building this in the open and we want collaborators, not just users.
